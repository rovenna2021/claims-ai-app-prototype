# Digital Claims Management Prototype with AI

## Overview

This project explores how product development, business architecture, and enterprise design thinking can accelerate the creation of a modern digital-first claims management platform with AI assistance for group insurance and health benefits.

The initiative focuses on improving:

* claims transparency,
* customer experience,
* operational efficiency,
* self-service enablement,
* and claims processing turnaround time.

Rather than approaching the problem purely as a UI exercise, this project intentionally combined:

* business architecture,
* operational workflow decomposition,
* capability mapping,
* product management,
* and rapid prototyping workflows

to design an enterprise-aligned digital claims experience.

The prototype demonstrates how complex operational domains can be translated into structured product specifications and interactive user experiences using product management and enterprise design principles and realized with the use of AI.

---

# Problem Statement

Traditional claims operations in insurance and health benefits often suffer from:

* fragmented customer experiences,
* lack of real-time claim visibility,
* inconsistent communications,
* operational silos,
* manual processing dependencies,
* and lengthy claims turnaround times.

Members frequently experience uncertainty around:

* what is covered,
* reimbursement expectations,
* claim status,
* payment timelines,
* and appeal outcomes.

Operationally, claims organizations must simultaneously manage:

* regulatory compliance,
* workflow orchestration,
* adjudication rules,
* fraud controls,
* provider coordination,
* financial settlement,
* and customer servicing.

This project explores how a digital-first, capability-driven approach could improve both:

* member experience,
* and operational effectiveness.

# Why Claims Management Is a Complex Enterprise Problem

Claims management is not simply a user interface problem.

It is a highly interconnected enterprise domain involving:

* policy and benefit rules,
* financial settlement operations,
* provider ecosystems,
* workflow orchestration,
* document management,
* compliance requirements,
* fraud controls,
* customer servicing,
* and operational performance optimization.

The challenge is balancing:

* operational efficiency,
* automation,
* compliance,
* explainability,
* transparency,
* and empathetic customer experiences simultaneously.

Claims workflows also contain significant variability:

* different claim types,
* varying adjudication paths,
* manual exceptions,
* retroactive adjustments,
* coordination of benefits,
* and appeal handling processes.

This project explored how business architecture and AI-assisted product workflows could help simplify and modernize that complexity while maintaining enterprise scalability and operational realism.

# How I Approached the Problem

Rather than starting with screens or UI components, I approached the problem through progressive decomposition.

## Step 1 — Understand Operational Pain Points

I first analyzed:

* claims operational challenges,
* customer pain points,
* transparency gaps,
* manual workflow bottlenecks,
* and claims servicing inefficiencies.

This helped frame the problem beyond just interface design.

## Step 2 — Define the Claims Value Stream

I structured the claims lifecycle into end-to-end value stream steps:

1. Coverage Inquiry
2. Claim Intake
3. Validation & Eligibility Check
4. Intelligent Triage
5. Claims Adjudication
6. Settlement & Payment
7. Adjustments & Corrections
8. Appeals & Dispute Resolution

This established the operational backbone of the platform.

## Step 3 — Define Personas

I defined personas across the claims ecosystem, including:

* digital-first members,
* providers,
* claims examiners,
* appeals specialists,
* and operations staff.

The prototype primarily focused on the Digital-First Member persona.

## Step 4 — Separate Capabilities from Workflows

I intentionally separated:

* business capabilities,
* workflow steps,
* platform capabilities,
* and product features.

This helped distinguish:

* reusable enterprise services,
* operational workflows,
* and user-facing functionality.

## Step 5 — Define Feature Hierarchy

Features were organized by:

* value stream alignment,
* capability ownership,
* and customer experience goals.

This enabled better traceability between:

* operational objectives,
* enterprise architecture,
* and user interactions.

## Step 6 — Model Use Cases and User Stories

I translated workflows into:

* use cases,
* user stories,
* and acceptance criteria

to create a more implementation-oriented product specification structure.

## Step 7 — Design Customer Journeys

I focused on high-value member journeys such as:

* checking coverage,
* submitting claims,
* uploading receipts,
* tracking claims,
* and monitoring payments.

## Step 8 — Prototype Key Experiences

Only after operational decomposition and workflow modeling did I move into:

* information architecture,
* screen flows,
* interaction design,
* and clickable prototype creation.

This helped ensure the prototype remained aligned to both:

* customer experience goals,
* and operational feasibility.

# Key Design Tradeoffs

Several architectural and operational tradeoffs emerged during the design process.

## Transparency vs Operational Complexity

Providing real-time claim visibility improves customer trust and reduces servicing calls, but requires orchestration across multiple backend operational states and systems.

## Automation vs Explainability

AI-assisted workflows can accelerate claims processing, but enterprise insurance environments require:

* explainability,
* auditability,
* and regulatory transparency.

This influenced the inclusion of:

* Explainable AI,
* operational audit trails,
* and workflow visibility concepts.

## Unified Experience vs Product Variability

Health, dental, and drug claims share similar intake experiences, but differ significantly in:

* adjudication logic,
* provider integrations,
* reimbursement models,
* and processing rules.

This led to a modular workflow approach with:

* shared intake capabilities,
* and specialized downstream processing.

## Self-Service vs Exception Handling

Digital-first workflows improve efficiency, but claims operations contain numerous exception scenarios requiring:

* manual review,
* escalation,
* and operational intervention.

The prototype therefore included:

* missing information workflows,
* escalation indicators,
* and manual review visibility.

## Product Definition Quality Strongly Impacts Prototype Quality

The clarity of:

* value streams,
* capabilities,
* use cases,
* and workflow boundaries

significantly affected downstream UX consistency and operational coherence.

## Enterprise Complexity Is Mostly About Coordination

Many operational challenges were less about individual screens and more about:

* workflow orchestration,
* system coordination,

## AI Works Best With Structured Inputs

AI-assisted workflows became significantly more effective when supported by:

* explicit operational models,
* structured decomposition,
* and clearly defined business context.

The experience reinforced that AI amplifies structured thinking more effectively than unstructured ideation.

## Capability Thinking Improves Product Scalability

Separating:

* workflows,
* capabilities,
* and features

helped clarify which services should remain reusable across multiple operational domains.
